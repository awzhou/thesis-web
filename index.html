Abstract

The oskar gene encodes an RNA and protein, both of which are highly localized within the developing oocyte and embryo and play important roles in Drosophila development. osk mRNA is localized to the posterior of the oocyte where it is translated into Oskar protein, which serves as the nucleator of germ granules within a specialized cytoplasm known as the germ plasm. These germ granules, which contain factors important to germline specification and development, are later incorporated into the primordial germ cells, known as pole cells in Drosophila. osk also has a noncoding function in the oocyte that is crucial for progression beyond the early stages of oogenesis. This noncoding function is mediated by the 3’ untranslated region, which contains several sequences in the so-called C region that are necessary for oogenesis progression. Various RNA-binding proteins, including Bruno, which suppresses translation of unlocalized osk, are known to bind to this region to contribute to osk noncoding function, but it is likely that others remain to be identified. Here, I identify a candidate gene that may have roles in osk noncoding function. Results from egg-laying assays and RNA immunoprecipitation experiments suggest that the RNA-binding protein Zinc finger protein at 72D (Zn72D) binds to osk RNA and contributes to its noncoding function. Zn72D also appears to play a role in the posterior localization of osk mRNA as shown in single molecule fluorescence in situ hybridization and RT-qPCR experiments with Zn72D knockdown flies. Knockdown of Zn72D continues to have effects later in the development of the germline. This is indicated in an oskar-sfgfp background by a decrease in the number of pole cells seen using immunohistochemical staining, which is consistent with the observed decrease in oskar posterior localization. This thesis proposes that Zn72D is a novel factor in both the non-coding function of oskar RNA and the localization of oskar mRNA to the posterior — where it is translated into Oskar protein — providing the groundwork for a deeper understanding of how the germline is specified.
